PEDIATRIC FLAIL CHEST

Goals

  1. To ensure for the patient with a chest inury a patent airway and adequate ventilatory mechanics.
  2. To ensure or restore the integrity of ventilation.
  3. To ensure or restore the circulatory status of the patient.
  4. To treat conditions specific to the chest injury.
  5. To ensure prompt access to a system of trauma care capable of addressing the patient's problem.

INDICATIONS:

  1. Patients following chest trauma who:
    a. Have tachypnea, dyspnea, or both.
    b. Display paradoxical motion of the chest wall, with a "floating segment".

EMT

  1. Focused history and physical exam.
  2. Assess ABC's for evidence of apnea, airway relex compromise or difficulty in ventilatory effort.
  3. Assess medical conditions, burns or traumatic injuries that have the potential to compromise the airway.
  4. Full set of Vital Signs including blood glucose.
  5. Provide bacsic airway maneuvers to all compromised airway, i.e. oxygen, jaw-thrust, and positioning.
  6. Identify and treat underlying reversible medical conditions (narcotic overdose, hypoglycemia, etc.).
  7. In general, maintain an oxygen saturation 94-99%.
  8. Always insure proper care of the C-Spine during Airway treament.
  9. Suction oral and nasal passages as needed to keep clear.
  10. Provide 100% oxygen to the patient.
  11. Ventilate with BVM when apneic or exhibiting respiratory distress. Consider a nasal or oral airway.
  12. Recommended pediatric ventilatory rates:
    • Infant (0-12 month): 25 breaths per minute.
    • 1-3 years: 20 breaths per minutes.
    • 4-6 years: 15 breaths per minute.
    • >6 years: 12 (same as adult).
    • Do not hyperventilate the patient.
  13. Apply direct pressure to any obvious external hemorrhage.
  14. Cover open chest wounds with occlusive dressing. Immobilize any obvious injuries and penetrating object, do not remove penetrating objects.
  15. Impaled objects should be stabilized in place and covered with dry sterile dressings. The exceptions would be:
    • Objects through the cheek where there is the possibility of airway compromise.
    • Objects that would interfere with chest compressions.
    • Sharp objects may need to be removed if their presence is cuasing ongoing injury, compromise, or inhibiting CPR.
  16. Tourniquets should be used to control hemorrhage not controlled with direct pressure and consider Protocol 512: EMS Tourniquet .
  17. Maintain warmth to minimize heat loss.
  18. Monitor for shock and hypovolemia.
  19. Assess mental status.
  20. STABILIZE flail segment - use pillow, folded sheet, towel and tape.

ADVANCED EMT

  1. Ensure above is complete.
  2. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available.
  3. Monitor for signs of tension pneumothroax.
  4. Decreasing bloood pressure associated with worsening dyspnea.
  5. Unilateral decreased breath sounds, increasing resistance to ventilation.
  6. Establish vascular access x2 if possible.
  7. Treat for pain and anxiety, consider Protocol 509: Pain Control/Nausea/Vomiting/Anxiety and contact OLMC for medication order:
    • Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg (max 25 mcg per dose) IV/IO.
    • In the event an IV cannot be established, Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg may be given IM up an initial dose of 25 mcg.
    • For additional doses, contact OLMC.
  8. If hypotensive bolus with NS 20 ml/kg then reassess - may give up to 60 ml/kg if not hypotensive TKO rate with a MAX bolus of 1 L.
  9. Initiate rapid TRANSPORT. Monitor for shock and hypovolemia. Assess mental status prior to and every 15 minutes during transport (GCS/AVPU).
  10. Contact Medical Control.

PARAMEDIC:

  1. Ensure the above is complete.
  2. INTUBATION- Consider orotracheal intubation using an endotracheal tube when indicated.
  3. Document TWO confirmation methods to varify endotracheal placement of ET tube (e.g. EtCO2, CO2 Detection Device or Esophogeal Intubation Detector.
  4. No nasotracheal intubation.

Medical Control Option-Must contact prior to administration.

  1. Suspected Tension Pneumothorax - Immediate needle decompression using appropriate technique.
  2. Traumatic Arrest- Consider bilateral eedled decompression for suspected tension pneumothroax based on mechanism of injury using appropriate technique.

ALTERED LEVEL OF CONSCIOUSNESS

The primary concern in a patient who has a serious alteration in consciousness is AIRWAY PROTECTION. Protective reflexes such as cough and gag are lost and aspiration is an ever present danger in these patients and can be lethal.
The Specific goals of protection are:

  1. To protect the patients from physical or environmental harm.
  2. To protect the patient's brain from injury due to lack of oxygen or glucose.
  3. To protect the patient from aspiration.
  4. To reverse several underlying causes of coma that may be present, such as hypoglycemia or opiate depression.
  5. To gather information for hospital personnel.

Key Considerations for all providers:

  1. Alteration in normal mental status including: confusion unusual behavior, or unresponsiveness to verbal or painful stimulus.
  2. Non-accidental trauma especially in pediatric and elderly patients.
  3. Hypoglycemia, especially in pediatric patients.
  4. Pediatric lowest acceptable systolic blood pressures are birth to 1 month =60 mmHg, 1 month to 1 year = 70 mmHg, 1 year to 10 years = 70 mmHg+(age x 2) and over 10 years = 90 mmHg.
  5. If poisoning suspected, contact OLMC, for guidance.
  6. Assess for possible overdoses, substance abuse or other potential toxin. Evaluate the scene for supportive evidence.
  7. Gather and collect any evidence on scene that may assist in the treatment of the paitent.
  8. A E I O U T I P P S: Possible causes of Altered Mental Status.
A-Alcohol T-Trauma/temp
E-Electrolytes I-Infection
I-Insulin P-Psychogenic
O-Opiates P-Poison
U-Uremia S-Shock/Seizure

EXCLUSIONS:

  1. Head injury or other trauma.
  2. Shock or serious cardiac arrhythmia.
  3. If any of above presents, go immediately to proper Protocol.

NOTE:

  1. Although alcohol is a common cause of alteration of consciousness, it is not commonly a cause of frank coma--total unresponsiveness to pain. In these protocols, NO JUDGEMENT IN THE FIELD IS TO BE MADE ON THE IMPORTANCE OF ALCOHOL AS THE CAUSE OF THE PRESENTING SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS. For practical purposes, and for the sake of the patient, no one is "just drunk".

EMT

  1. ASSESS LEVEL OF CONSCIOUSNESS and maintain AIRWAY as indicated.
  2. Focused history and physical exam.
  3. Blood glucose, oxygen saturation and temperature assessment.
  4. Assess for trauma.
  5. Assess for stroke and score using CSS consider PROTOCOL 307: Suspected CVA-TIA.
  6. Place patient in left lateral recumbent position to prevent aspiration. (Full spinal stabilization precautions considered).
  7. SUCTION as necessary
  8. ASSIST VENTILATION.
  9. If oxygen is applied use NC if possible to keep sat >94%, if unable to obtain then a nonreabreather mask is used.
  10. Anticipate vomiting and ensure the airway is monitored and protected at all times.

ADVANCED EMT

  1. Ensure all above is completed.
  2. Continuous ECG monitor, ETCO2, and Pulse oximetry monitoring when available.
  3. Start IV with NS TKO or saline lock unless hypotensive (systolic <100) then give 250 ml bolus may repeat x 1.
  4. Obtain a 12 kead ECG if availbale.
  5. CHECK GLUCOMETER READING - IF BELOW 60 mg/dl then administer 25 gm of Dextrose D50 . IV PUSH. If no IV access after 2 attempts, consider Glucagon 1 mg IM.
  6. CHECK FOR Narcotic Ingestion (respiratory depression, history of narcotic usage, constricted pupils, drug vials or paraphenalia near patient), If any of these present, give Naloxone 1-2 mg (per dose) IV/IM/IO/ or intranansal (atomizer). May repeat x 1
  7. Consider physical restraints as needed to protect the patient and/or personnel.
  8. Contact OLMC: Consider Protocol 803: Restraints Physical - Violent Patient/Chemical Sedation.

PARAMEDIC

  1. Ensure all above is completed.
  2. INTUBATE if paitent remains unresponsive without gag after initial treatment unless Advanced airway is in place.
  3. TRANSPORT, perform and document frequent vitals and neurological exams.
  4. CONSULT Medical control.

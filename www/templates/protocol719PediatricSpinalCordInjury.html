PEDIATRIC SPINAL CORD INJURY

Goals

  1. Protection of the patient against further injury.
  2. Maintenance of vital functions in patients with suspected or actual spinal cord injury.
  3. Maintenance of perfusion to the damaged spinal cord to reduce changes of edema and further insult.
  4. Data recording for serial evaluation of the patient.
  5. Psychological support in the early management of the patient with spinal cord injury.
  6. Facilitation of entry of the patient into a trauma system designated to care for these patients.

INDICATIONS:

  1. Patents with blunt or penetrating trauma near or in area of spinal column.
  2. Patients with numbness, weakness or other neurologic deficits after trauma.
  3. Mechanism suggestive of potential spine injury such as:
    • MVA
    • Bicycle accident.
    • Blunt facial trauma.
    • Fall.
    • GSW near the midline.

Key Considerations:

  1. Spinal Immobilization criteria - Immobilize patient with cervival collar and spine board if there is a traumatic mechanism of injury and any of the below criteria are met.
  2. Spinal immobilization should be considered a treatment or prevententive therapy.
  3. Patients who are likely to benefit from immobilization should undergo this treatment.
  4. Patients may not need immobilized providing they do not meet any of the spinal immobilization criteria as listed above and the provider's clinical judgment also determines no need for immobilization.
  5. Ambulatory patients who are alert and cooperative may be safely immobilized on a gurney with cervical collar and straps and will not generally require a spine board unless they meet any of the spinal immobilization criteria as listed:
    • Age <8.
    • Patient complains of neck or spine pain.
    • There is any nexk or spinal tenderness.
    • There is any abnormal mental staus or GCS <15.
    • There is any evidence of alcohol or drug intoxication.
    • There are other severely painful or disctracting injuries present.
      CONTACT OLMC FOR FURTHER INSTRUCTIONS IF THE PATIENT OR PATIENT'S GUARDIAN REFUSES IMMOBILIZATION DESPITE THE PROVIDER'S ASSESSMENT FOR THE NEED FOR SPINAL IMMOBILIZIATION.

ALL PROVIDERS

  1. Focused history and physical exam.
  2. Full set of Vital Signs including blood glucose.
  3. Evaluate the mechanism of injury.
  4. Assess exposure to drugs, alcohol or other toxins including environmental toxins.
  5. Assess history of arthritis, cancer, or other possible spine/bone diseases.
  6. Assess environment, location of patient, and need for extrication.
  7. If spinal immobilization is to be applied:
    • Explain the need for spinal immobilization to the patient.
    • Apply appropriate cervical immobilization.
    • Apply appropriate backboard and security straps.
    • PEDS - Use a pediatric specific backboard for those <8 years old OR use a towel or pad to raise the child's body (not their haead) to ensure appropriate spinal alignment on an adult board.
    • Age <2 should be immobilized in a car seat or age appropriate papoose device.
    • Assess neurological fuction before, during, and after application of spinal immobilization.
    • Children who are <5 years old should be immobilized until evaluation at the hospital if the mechanism is suspicious.
    • Children <8 years old cannot have their C-Spine cleared in the field.
  8. Follow Appropriate Protocol 700: Pediatric Assessment series as needed based on applicable trauma guidelines.

PEDIATRIC AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION

All Providers

A patient in cardiac or respiratory arrest in a restaurant or where food is being served, or with a history of foreign body ingestion (i.e., recently playing with toys, marbles, or food), should be presumed to have a foreign body upper airway obstruction. The Heimlich maneuver must be performed or the upper airway visualized with direct larygoscopy as soon as possible.

INDICATIONS:

  1. All patients who CANNOT SPEAK, or are cyanotic, and are suspected of foreign body upper airway obstruction.
  2. All patients in cardiac arrest that occurred in a restaurant or during a meal
  3. Determine responsiveness and whether or not the child can cough or speak.

PATIENT CONSCIOUS - ALL PROVIDERS

Focused history and physical exam.

  1. Assess ABC's for evidence of apnea, airway reflex compromise or difficulty in ventilatroy effort.
  2. Assess medical conditions, burns or traumatic injuries that have the potential to compromise the airway.
  3. Provide basic airway maneuvers to all compromised airways, i.e. oxygen, jaw-thrust, and positioning.
  4. Identify and treat underlying reversible medical conditions (narcotic overdoes, hypoglycemia, etc.).
  5. In general, maintain and oxygen saturation 90-99% and ETCO2 of 35-45 mmHg.
  6. Always insure proper care of the C-Spine during Airway treatment.
  7. Keep the patient NPO-Stop any tube feedings and do not use feeding tube during resuscitation.
  8. Infants and young children are primary nose breathers-Suction oral and nasal passages as needed to keep clear.
  9. Tracheostomy/Home Ventilator:
    a. Primary caretakers and families are the best resource for understanding the equpment they are using.
    b. Disconnect the ventilator and assist ventilations with BVM if the patient is apneic, unresponsive, or has severe respiratory distress or depression.
    c. If unable to ventilate, suction the tracheostomy, then reattempt ventilatory efforts.
  10. If still unable to ventilate, attempt traditional BVM (i.e. place a gloved finger over the trach to occlude during the delivery of a breath).
  11. Provide 100% oxygen to the paitent.
  12. Ventilate with BVM when apneic or exhibiting respiratory distress.
  13. Consider a nasal or oral airway.
  14. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available.
  15. Recommended pediatric ventilatory rates:
    a. Infants (0-12 months): 25 breaths per minutes.
    b. 1-3 years: 20 breaths per minutes.
    c. 4-6 years: 15 breaths per minutes.
    d. >6 years: 12 (Same as adult).
    e. Do not hyperventilate the patient.
  16. Encourage coughing, if ineffective:
    a. CHILD > 1 YEAR, perform the Heimlich maneuver, (Subdiaphragmatic abdominal thrusts) in a series of 5 thrusts and repeat until effective or until the patient becomes unconscious.
    b. INFANTS of CHILDREN < 1 YEAR, straddle the infant over the rescuer's arems in a prone position with the head lower than the trunk and support the head.
    c. Deliver 5 back blows with the heel ofthe hand between the infant's shoulder blades.
    d. Turn the infant onto the supine position, keep the head lower than the trunk and give 5 chest thrusts (same location as CPR.) Repeat until effective or patient becomes unconscious.

PATIENT UNCONSCIOUS - ALL PROVIDERS

  1. Perform Heimlich maneuver (5 thrusts) in CHILDREN >1 YEAR.
  2. 5 back blows/5 chest thrusts in CHILDREN < 1 YEAR.
  3. DO NOT USE BLIND FINGER SWEEPS!
  4. Open airway and reattempt to ventilate.
  5. If unable to ventilate, repeat steps 1 and 2 until successful.

ADVANCED EMT

  1. Ensure above is complete.
  2. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available.
  3. Establis IV access if able.

PARAMEDIC

  1. Ensure above is compete.
  2. If the object in not expelled and a laryngoscope is available, visulaize the upper airway with a larygoscope and remove the foreign body with Magill forcepts.
  3. INTUBATION- Consider orotracheal intubation using an endotracheal tube when indicated:
    a. Document TWO confirmation methods to verify endotracheal placement of ET tube (e.g. EtCO2, CO2 Detection Device or Esophageal Intubation Detector).
    b. Consider sedation after intubation (benzodiazepine)-contact OLMC.
    c. After 2 unsuccessful attempts at endotracheal intubation use a superglottic airway device or BVM with appropriate oral/nasal airway.
    d. No nasotracheal intubation.

  4. Ventilator Management.
    a. Work with the family to troubleshoot the machine.
    b. Address thracheostomy as above.
    c. If you need to disconnect for transport provide adequate BVM ventilations similar to home respiratory rate settings.
    d. Contact OLMC for further instructions as needed.

Medical Control Option - must contact prior to administration

  1. Sedation after intubation (benzodiazepine).
  2. Surgical Airway - Cricothyrotomy.
    a. Consider only when all other methods of oxygenation, ventilation and securing the airway have failed.
    b. Insert an appropriately sized endotracheal tube and secure.
  3. Tracheostomy Assistance:
    a. Provide supplemental oxygen.
    b. Suction the patient appropriately.
    c. Replace Tracheostomy tube if needed.
    d. IF unable to ventilate, pass an appropriately asized ETT through the stoma 1-2 inches,
    e. If unable to pass a tracheostomy tube or endotracheal tube, use BVM, supraglottic airway device, or orothracheal intubation to ventilate patient.
    f. Contact OLMC for cuther instructions.

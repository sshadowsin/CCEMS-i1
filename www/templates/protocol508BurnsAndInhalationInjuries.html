BURNS AND INHALATION INJURIES

ALL PROVIDERS

1. SCENE SAFETY IS THE #1 PRIORITY! ALWAYS ENSURE THE SCENE IS SAFE PRIOR TO ANY INTERVERNTIONS.
2. Electrical Burns are frequently more serious than they appear.
3. Identifying the source of AC or DC voltage with the amperage will be helpful in the treatment.
4. Burn patients are TRAUMA patients. Care for traumatic injuries should precede care for the burn.
5. Potential CO and/or Cyanide exposure (closed space smoke exposure) should receive 100% oxygen, by NRB.
6. Patients are prone to hypothermia due to heat loss from the burns.
7. Consider Child Abuse as a cause. Circumferential scald burn to hands, feet, buttocks, and genitalia are common burns seen in child abuse (especially in children <5 years old).

EMT

  1. Focused history and physical exam.
  2. In the unconscious patient, implement spinal motion restriction.
  3. Remove all prostheses, rings, and constricting bands from extermities.
  4. Closely monitor patient's temperature and prevent hypothermia.
  5. Identify potential entry and exit wounds for electrical burns - both sites will generally be a full thickness burn site.
  6. Thermal Burns:
    a. Stop the burning process.
    b. Do not pull material out of the wound but cut clothing around it.
  7. Electrical Burns:
    a. Safely evacuate patient from electrical source.
    b. Do not touch the patient until you are sure that the electrical source is disconnected.
    c. When multiple paitents are struck simultaneously by lightning or a high voltage source, those in respiratory and/or cardiac arrest should be given the highest priority of care, even those who appear dead or initial evaluation.
  8. OXYGEN non-rebreather mask at 15L ASSIST VENTILATION as needed, monitor closely for airway edema.
  9. If patient was in enclosed space or in proximity to explosion/flashback, assume CO exposure and potential inhalation injury.
  10. Monitor closely for airway edema.
  11. Apply burn free.
Treatment of Burns:
  1. Dressings:
    a. Partial or Full Thickness (2nd or 3rd degree) <10% BSA - Wet sterile dressings.
    b. Partial or Full Thickness (2nd or 3rd degree) >10% BSA - Dry sterile dressings.
Definitions:
  1. Superficial (1st degree) Burns _ red painful, without blisters.
  2. Partial Thickness (2nd degree) Burns - red, painful/hypersensitive, swollen, with either intact or ruptured blisters.
  3. Full Thickness (3rd degree) Burns - dark, leathery, painless, waxy and does not blanch.
  4. Estimate size and deth of burn using the percentage chart (below)

ADVANCED EMT

  1. Ensure above is complete.
  2. Continuous ECG, ETC02, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available, avoid placing monitor attachments over burned skin if possible.
  3. 12 lead ECG where appropriate and if possible, avoid placing electrodes over burned skin.
  4. Monitor for developing airway compromise.
  5. With electrical burns anticipate heart rhythm irregularities (both ventricular and artial).
  6. Assess for circulatory compromise from circumferential extremity burns or ventilatory compromise from circumferential chest burns.
  7. INITIATE TRANSPORT
  8. IV NS large bore at TKO unless hypotensive then give 500 ml bolus and reassess. Titrate to keep vital signs within normal limits. If possible, avoid placing IV through burned skin.
  9. Partial or Full Thickness (2nd or 3rd degree) >10% BSA - flush therapy following Parkland Burn Forumla: NS 4 ml per kg body weight per % burn during the first 24 hours.
    a. To calcuate: multiply 4 ml X kg X % burn = total fluid requirement.
    b. Give half of this amount during the first 8 hours from the time of injury.
  10. Contact Medical Control.
  11. Treat for pain and anxiety: Protocol 509: Pain Control, Nausea, Vomiting, Anxiety

PARAMEDIC

  1. Ensure above is complete.
  2. INTUBATE as appropriate.
  3. If patient becomes agitated with increasing LOC after intubation may give Versed 2-5 mg slow IV with Medical Control permission.
  4. Consult Medical control if need for surgical airway appears imminent.
  5. High voltage electrical injury or direct lightning strike with significant tissue destruction: Sodium Bicarb 1 mEq/kg (maximum of 100 mEq) in 1000 mL NS wide open.

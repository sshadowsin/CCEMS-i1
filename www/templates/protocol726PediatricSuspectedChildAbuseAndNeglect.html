PEDIATRIC SUSPECTED CHILD ABUSE AND NEGLECT

INDICATIONS (WHEN TO SUSPECT):

  1. HISTORY - RED FLAGS!
    • History of injury incompatible with type of degree of injury.
    • History of vague, parent reluctant to give history or contradictory histories given by each parent.
    • History impossible for childish level of development.
    • Significant and inappropriate delay between time of injury and time you were called.
    • You have been repeatedly called to this home for ingestions and/or injuries and may have been asked to take the child to a different hospital each time.
    • You have been repeatedly called to this home about alarming chief complaints and find a well infant (this may be a "pre-abuse" situation).
  2. BEHAVIOR - RED FLAGS!
    • Parent doesn't show concern appropriate to the child's injury.
    • Parent shows inappropriate expectation of the child.
    • Parent makes inappropriate demands of the child.
    • Parent demonstrates angry impulsive behavior toward the child.
  3. PHYSICAL - RED FLAGS!
    • Patterns of injury:
      • Bruises in unusual location (e.g. back, buttocks, face, ears, upper arms, thighs, hands, feet).
      • Surface marks suggestion a weapon (e.g. belt, looped cord, switch, rope or chain).
      • Burns (e.g. immersion scalds, large imprints of an iron, radiator, heater or cigarette).
      • Long bone (especially humerus or femur) and/or hip fractures in children uner 2 years of age with no reasonable history.
      • Skull fractures or signs suggesting CNS ingury (especially in an infant with no reasonable history).
      • Signs suggestive of significant abdominal, perineal or chest injury without a reasonable hisrly.
      • Signs of failure to thrive (thin or wasted infant or toddler), often dirty and apathetic.
      • Non-accidental trauma can occur in patients of any age and in all ethnic and socio-economic groups.
      • Risk factors include children under age of 5, the elderly, drug and alcohol abuse and a history of domestic violence.
      • In children under the age of two the most common form of child abuse is Abusive Head Injury (AHI). Mortality of AHI is 25%. For those that live, there is significant morbidity, usually associated with traumatic brain injury.
      • Do not directly engage a hostile patient, parent, assailant or perpertrator. If situation becomes unsafe for EMS personnel, call for police assistance.

MOST LIKELY SITUATIONS:

  1. Severe acute injury with loss of consciousness, seizure or cardiovascular collapse.
  2. Sudden decompensation in an infant with prior head or abdominal injury.
  3. Sudden decompensation in an infant with failure to thrive due to dehydration or sepsis.
  4. Severe symptoms due to ingestion.

EMT/ADVANCED EMT/PARAMEDIC

  1. Contact law enforcement if someone on scene is a threat to themselves or others.
  2. Separate any possible assailants, including parents, from the patient.
  3. Remove patient from the stressful environmrent and remove any possible weapons.
  4. Non-accidental trauma includes any act of commission or omission that results in harm to a person's physical, developmental, or emotional state.
  5. Focused history and physical exam.
  6. Blood glucose, Temperature and Oxygen Saturation assessment.
  7. Always consider the possibility of abuse when evaluating any medical condition or trauma.
  8. Suspect: Look for suspicious circumstances or actions from paitent or caregiver.
    • Listen to and document circumstances of the event.
    • Evaluate the enviorment in which you find the patient.
  9. Protect: Be the patient advocate.
    • Make all efforts to remove the patient from the situation.
  10. Respect: Communicate appropriately with family.
    • Avoid confrontation with caregiver.
    • Confrontation may lead to caregiver's refusal to allow you to care for thre patient.
    • Be nonjudgmental and avoid accusations.
    • Consider law enforcement assistance.
  11. Collect:
    • Document using direct quotation when possible.
    • Document objectively without speculation.
  12. Report: You have the responsibility to report suspected abuse and neglect to the ED and also to the law enforcement.
  13. Stabilize: Apply appropriate Protocol as indicated by the patient's condition.
  14. Observe: Conditions/features of the enviromnent and parental behavior that may be helpful in establishing a diagnosis of abuse or neglect: evidence of violence or disarray, frightened/bruised mother or siblings, aggressive or inappropriate behavior of parents or other household member.
  15. BE SYMPATHETIC AND SUPPORTIVE towars parents and child.

NOTE:

DO NOT MENTION THAT YOU SUSPECT ABUSE. DO NOT ACT IN A JUDGMENTAL MANNER OR CONFRONT THE PARENTS IN ANY WAY. This will only inflame them and may subject the field team members and the patient to significant physical risk.

ADVANCED EMT (CCEMS) and PARAMEDIC:

  1. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when avaiable.
  2. If anxious or agitated, attempt non-pharmacological options to calm the patient.
  3. Establish vascular access if possible.
  4. Treat for pain and anxiety, consider Protocol 509: Pain Control/Nausea/Vomiting/Anxiety and contact OLMC for medication order:
    a. Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg (max 50 mcg per dose) IV/IO
    b. In the event an IV cannot be established, Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg may be given IM up an initial dose of 25 mcg.
    c. For additional doses, contact OLMC
  5. TRANSPORT the patient to the hospital.
  6. DOCUMENT patients observations and comments.

Medical Control Option-Must contact prior to administration.

  1. PEDIATRIC ANXIETY CONTROL:
    • Midazolam (Versed) :
      • Dosage is cut in half if the patient has received narcotics or alcohol.
      • Consider the size of the patient for dosing.
      • IV/IO - 0.1 mg/kg (max dose of 4 mg per dose).
      • Do NOT exceed adult dosing.
      • Intranasal or oral - 0.2 mg/kg (max dose 10mg per dose).
      • Contact OLMC for additional doses.

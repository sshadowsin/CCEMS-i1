PEDIATRIC ASSESSMENT

All Providers

  1. The pediatric assessment should be modified for the developmental level of each patient.
  2. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available.
  3. Treatment Plan (develop and implement plan based on assessment findings, resources, and training).
  4. Use the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (defined by the AAP) to form a general impression of the child.
  1. Appearance: Evaluate tone, interactiveness, consolability, gaze, and speech or cry.
  2. Breathing: Evaluate abnormal airway sounds, abnormal positioning, retractions, and nasal flaring.
  3. Circulation/Skin Color: Evaluate for pallor, mottling, and cyanosis.
    a. If the patient looks ill, start CPR when the heart rate is less than:
    i. 80 bpm for infants (up to 1 year of age).
    ii. 60 bpm for chldren (1 year to 8 years).
    b. Look on scene for a CHIRP red bag. It contains current medical information on the child with special healthcare needs Protocol 727: Pediatric Special Heath Care Needs.
    c. Perform the pediatric assessment with guidance from Protocol 913: Family Centered Care.
    d. Pay careful attention to the wide variety of normal vital signs. Do not assume that the pediatric patient is fine when they have vitals meeting the normal adult parameters.

Key Considerations:

  1. Obtaining a full set of vital signs including blood pressures should be a priority.
  2. Parents are often the best resource for a baseline understanding of their child, especially in the case of the child with special healthcare needs.

SNAKE BITES

ALL PROVIDERS

  1. Focused history and physical exam.
  2. Identify and document the type of snake, appearance, location, and distinguishing marks.
  3. Obtain an accurate time of injury.
  4. Clarify any first aid provided by friends or family prior to arrival.
  5. Common venomous snake common to Utah are: The Great Basin rattlesnake, the Hopi rattlesnake, and the Mojave rattlesnake.
  6. Many bites are "dry" and do not have venom injected-monitor for signs of envenomation.
  7. Many exotic reptiles especially snakes may be kept as pets so always be cautious.
  8. Signs of envenomation include paresthesia, metallic taste, chills, nausea, vomiting, headache, dysphagia, cramps, hypotension, fever, local edema, blebs, and discoloration.
  9. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse oximetry monitoring when available.

Treatment Plan

  1. Ensure scene safety by moving the patient to a safe distance, away from the snake.
  2. Splint limb and place below the level of the heart.
  3. Keep patient calm and movement to a minimum.
  4. Remove items that may constrict swelling tissue.
  5. DO NOT APPLY TOURNIQUET, CUT THE WOUND OR USE ANY COMMERCIAL SNAKE BITE KITS.
  6. Provide 100% oxygen to the patient.
  7. Ventilate with BVM when apneic or exhibiting respiratory distress. Consider a nasal or oral airway.
  8. Maintain a ventilatory rate of 10-12 breaths per minute for Adults.
  9. Recommended pediatric ventilatory rates:
    a. Infant (0-12 months): 25 breaths per minute
    b. 1-3 yrs.: 20 breaths per minute
    c. 4-6 yrs.: 15 breaths per minute
    d. >6 years: 12 (Same as adult)
  10. Do not hyperventilate the patient.
  11. Do not start the IV in the affected limb.
  12. Do not apply ice to the limb.
  13. Do not try to capture the snake.
  14. Do not bring a live snake to the ED.
  15. If you transport the snake make sure you do it safely. Remember that snakes can reflexively envenomate up to 1 hour after death, keep in a sealed plastic or metal container.
  16. Pictures of the snake can be helpful for identification-obtain a series of pictures if possible.
  17. Any bite can be dangerous and should be evaluated in the ED.
  18. Watch for signs of shock and allergic reaction.

ADVANCED EMT/PARAMEDIC

  1. Ensure above is complete.
  2. Transport-notify OLMC so that antivenom can be on standby.
  3. Continuous ECG, ETCO2, and Pulse Oximetry monitoring when available.
  4. Maintain an oxygen saturation 90-94% and ETCO2 of 35-45 mmHg
  5. Keep the patient NPO
  6. Establish vascular access x 2 if able-set at TKO rate unless hypotensive then give NS boluses of 250-500 ml for adult or 20 ml/kg for pediatric patients up to a max of 60 ml/kg.
  7. Pain-anxiety control considering Protocol 509: Pain Control, Nausea, Vomiting, Anxiety
  8. If allergic rection noted follow Protocol Protocol 310: Allergic Reaction/Anaphylaxis (Adult) or Protocol 704: Pediatric Anaphylaxis (Pediatric)
  9. Advanced airway as applicable.
